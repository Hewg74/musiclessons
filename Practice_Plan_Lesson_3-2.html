Lesson 3/2 — Sarah Glass  |  7-Day Cycle  |  Baritone, Break ≈ A3
What This Lesson Is Actually About

Sarah is building your internal clock — the ability to feel subdivisions in your body, not just your head. The nod exercise isn't a warm-up gimmick: it's training proprioceptive rhythm, where downbeat lives in your neck/torso and upbeat lives in the lift. This is how session musicians internalize groove without thinking.

Every exercise in this lesson connects back to this: the metronome drills build the clock, the song work tests whether it holds under cognitive load (lyrics, chord changes, syncopation).

The vocal AI work is about passaggio control — specifically navigating your mix/head flip around A3–B♭3 with enough control that you can use it musically rather than just surviving it.

4-Month Progression Arc

Aug–Sep 2025: Island strum fundamentals. Rhythm counting on A chord. "I Like The Way You Walk" melody note identification. Vocal app work for pitch accuracy. Piano keys: Northside at 69 BPM, F#m pentatonic.

Feb 2026: Groove beat work at 90 BPM. AI vocal exercises. "I Like The Way You Walk" timing analysis — syncopation on last word of each line.

Mar 2026 (NOW): Metronome internalization with physical nod. Subdivision stripping (full count → &'s only → 16ths). Singing with metronome for rhythmic lyric placement. Sol Del Sur lead + strum. Passaggio flip control.

The through-line: rhythm → embodied rhythm → rhythm under cognitive load → rhythm as the vehicle for musical expression.

Pre-Practice Protocol (Do This Every Day)

Sarah has mentioned these across multiple lessons. This isn't optional — it protects your voice and primes your body for the work.

Before You Touch the Guitar (~8 min)

1

Hydration Check

16oz water minimum 30 min before practice. Avoid dairy before singing (Sarah, Lesson 8/27: "Avoid dairy, octave slides with lip roll or tongue roll"). Dairy thickens mucus on the vocal folds.

2

Lip Trills / Tongue Trills (2 min)

Start with a comfortable pitch (around D3), do a slow siren up to D4 and back down. 4–5 rounds. This is a semi-occluded vocal tract (SOVT) exercise — it creates backpressure that gently stretches and warms the vocal folds without strain. If lip trills are hard, use "vvv" on the same pattern.

3

Octave Slides on Lip Roll (2 min)

Starting on G2, slide up a full octave to G3 on a lip roll, then back down. Move up chromatically: A♭2→A♭3, A2→A3, B♭2→B♭3. When you hit the A2→A3 slide, notice if the trill gets harder or uneven near the top — that's your passaggio. Don't push through it; just observe.

4

Straw Phonation (2 min)

Sing through a cocktail straw (or coffee stirrer) on a sustained "oo." Start at F3, hold 5 sec, move to G3, A♭3, A3. The straw creates even more backpressure than lip trills — it's the best diagnostic for whether your vocal folds are warmed up. If A3 feels strained through the straw, you need more warm-up time before working the break.

5

Gentle Humming (1 min)

Hum a simple melody (the first line of "I Like The Way You Walk" works) in your comfortable mid-range. Feel the vibration in your face/mask. This primes forward placement for the singing work ahead.

Vowel Modification Cheat Sheet

This is the missing piece for your passaggio work. As you approach A3 from below, your vowels need to gradually close/cover to allow mixed voice to engage:

VOWEL MODIFICATION MAP (Baritone, break ≈ A3) E3 F3 F#3 G3 A♭3 A3 B♭3 B3 C4 │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ │ ├─────┴─────┴─────┘ │ │ │ │ │ "ah" stays open │ │ │ │ │ ┌─────┘ │ │ │ │ "ah" → "uh" │ │ │ │ ┌─────┘ │ │ │ "uh" → "oh" │ │ │ ┌─────┴─────┘ │ "oh" stays │ (full head voice zone) │ PRACTICAL: Singing "ah" on G3? Start adding a hint of "uh" roundness. Singing "ah" on A3? It should sound more like "aw" or "uh." Singing "ee" on A3? Let it drift toward "ih." WHY: Closing the vowel lowers the first formant, which lets your vocal folds thin out for mixed/head voice instead of slamming into chest voice ceiling.
7-Day Execution Plan

Day 1: Foundation

Metronome Internalization  |  50–55 min
START HERE

10 min Metronome Drill #1: Full Count

  • Metronome at 200 BPM (building to 244 by mid-week)
  • Say: 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & — every click = one syllable
  • Tap tempo at HALF speed (100–122) — tap only on numbers
  • NOD down on numbers, up-nod on &'s
  • Do 4–8 bars, rest 10 sec, repeat 3–4 sets
Sarah says: This is the most important exercise. Always start here. The nod is non-negotiable — it's proprioceptive anchoring. If the nod drifts, slow the metronome by 20 BPM. Feel it in your neck, not just your hand.

8 min Metronome Drill #2: &'s Only

  • Same setup: metronome at 200–244, tap at 100–122
  • Only say the &'s out loud (silent on numbers)
  • Keep the nod on the numbers!
  • Your body holds the downbeat while your voice speaks the offbeat
This is the hardest drill. The nod IS your downbeat anchor. If you lose the nod, you've lost the exercise. This is training internal pulse independence.

8 min Metronome Drill #3: 16th Note Feel

  • Metronome at 78 BPM, tap at 78
  • Say: 1 e and a 2 e and a 3 e and a 4 e and a
  • Then strip to only saying "e and a" (silent on numbers)
  • 4 even syllables per beat
Don't rush the "e and a" — space them perfectly evenly. This feeds directly into syncopation awareness for the songs.

12 min Surf Rock Fingerpicking (120 BPM)

  • Am → C → G → D, fingerpick pattern
  • Count 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & out loud while playing
  • Alternate thumb/pointer on two strings — count is on the thumb
  • 4 thumb-pointer cycles per chord
  • WITH THE NOD the entire time
Counting out loud while fingerpicking = cognitive load test. If the count drops, simplify the picking until the count is solid. Priority: count > nod > picking accuracy.

12 min Vocal Work: Passaggio Warm-Up

  • Exercise 1: Descending 5-note scales through A3 (see Vocal tab)
  • Exercise 2: Messa di voce on G3–A3–B♭3
  • Keep volume conversational — don't push

5 min Listening Homework

  • Listen to "I Like The Way You Walk" by The Donkeys — no guitar, just listen
  • Focus on where his last word of each line falls relative to the beat
  • Is it on a beat? Between beats? Late?
Sarah has flagged this timing issue three lessons in a row. Analytical listening without playing is how you internalize it.
✓ Level-up marker: You can hold 4 consecutive bars of Drill #1 at 220+ BPM with a clean nod without losing count.

Vocal Exercises for Baritone Passaggio

Designed for a baritone with a primary break around A3 (A3–B♭3). These integrate with Sarah's lesson themes: rhythm internalization, pitch matching, dynamic improvisation, and mixed/head voice flip control.

Your passaggio means chest voice tops out around G3–A3, and head voice begins to engage around A3–B♭3. The goal isn't to eliminate the break — it's to build a functional mixed voice that lets you transition smoothly. Think of it as a manual transmission with a sticky clutch. We're making it an automatic.

Exercise 1: Descending 5-Note Scale Through the Break

Approach the break from ABOVE (easier than ascending through it)

Starting note: C4. Sing a descending 5-note scale (C4–B3–A3–G3–F3) on "nee" or "nay." The nasal consonant maintains forward placement through the flip.

Descend chromatically each round: start on C4, then B3, then B♭3, then A3, then A♭3. You'll feel the flip happen in the B♭3–A3 zone on the way down.

Round 1: C4 → B3 → A3 → G3 → F3 (on "nee") Round 2: B3 → A#3 → G#3 → F#3 → E3 Round 3: Bb3 → A3 → Ab3 → G3 → Gb3 ← flip zone Round 4: A3 → Ab3 → G3 → Gb3 → F3 ← deep in it Round 5: Ab3 → G3 → Gb3 → F3 → E3 Metronome: 80 BPM, one note per beat. Volume: conversational 6/10. Don't push.
Do 5–6 rounds. Don't force chest up or pull head down — let the voice do what it wants and observe where it flips. The observation IS the exercise.

Exercise 2: Messa di Voce on Break Notes

Dynamic control right at the passaggio — the #1 exercise for building mixed voice

Pick a single note in your break zone: start with G3. Sing on "ah." Start quiet (pp), swell to medium-loud (mf), then back to quiet. One cycle ≈ 8 seconds.

Do 3 cycles on G3, then move to A♭3, A3, B♭3.

pp ──────── mf ──────── pp (8 sec total) │ │ quiet → swell → quiet G3: 3 cycles (this should feel easy) A♭3: 3 cycles (getting interesting) A3: 3 cycles (the flip zone — the crescendo will try to flip you) B♭3: 3 cycles (let it flip if it wants to)

Vowel modification applies here: As you crescendo on A3, let "ah" drift toward "uh." The closing vowel enables the mixed voice mechanism.

Breath: Use appoggio — maintain outward expansion of the lower ribs even as you exhale. Think of "leaning into" the air column, not squeezing. The diaphragm stays engaged throughout the swell. This is what gives you dynamic control without strain.

By the time you reach B♭3, you'll likely notice the voice wants to flip on the crescendo. Let it. The goal is to make that flip smaller and more controlled over time — not to eliminate it.

Exercise 3: Siren Glides (Pitch Mapping)

Map your entire range and find exact flip points

On a lip trill (or "vvv"), glide from your lowest comfortable note (~C2–D2) all the way to your highest comfortable head voice (~E4–G4), then back down. Smooth, no jumps.

Speed: 4–5 seconds up, 4–5 seconds down. Do 5 rounds.

On rounds 3–5, slow down through the A3 zone. Really feel what's happening at the flip. Is it a sudden crack or a gradual shift? The lip trill reduces subglottic pressure, making the flip gentler and easier to observe.

Record yourself. Listen for where the timbre changes. That's your passaggio. Over weeks, the crack should become a gradual shift.

Exercise 4: Rhythmic Pitch Matching

Pitch accuracy + rhythmic internalization — ties directly to the lesson

Use the Groove Beat at 90 BPM. Play Am on guitar. Sing:

Bar 1: Root (A2) on beat 1 → 3rd (C3) on beat 3 Bar 2: 5th (E3) on beat 1 → Octave (A3) on beat 3 Bar 3: 5th (E3) on beat 1 → 3rd (C3) on beat 3 Bar 4: Root (A2) on beat 1 → rest Then change chord (C): Bar 5: C3 on beat 1 → E3 on beat 3 Bar 6: G3 on beat 1 → C4 on beat 3 ...and so on for G and D.

The intervals train pitch matching to specific chord tones while maintaining rhythmic placement. You're never just "hitting notes" — you're placing them inside the beat.

Once comfortable: try placing notes on the &'s instead of the beats. Same pitches, but now they're syncopated. This directly trains what ILTWYW requires.

Exercise 5: Improvised "Ooh" Climbing

Dynamic improvisation + passaggio navigation — from Lesson 1/27

Surf Rock Beat at 120 BPM. Am C G D, island strum or fingerpick. On each chord, sing 4 "ooh" notes that climb. Different pattern every chord. Start in chest voice; as you cycle through repetitions, let the climbing notes push into and through your break.

Rules from Sarah: (1) Not the same 4 notes every chord. (2) COMMIT — more air pressure = better pitch accuracy. (3) If a note cracks, that's data, not failure.

Vowel trick: As you climb past G3, shift "ooh" toward a slightly more open "uh." This is the same vowel modification principle — it lets your voice navigate the passaggio instead of slamming into it.

Variation: Try "mah" or "nah" instead of "ooh." The consonant onset helps with pitch accuracy and forward placement. The nasal consonants (m, n) are particularly good for passaggio work because they naturally encourage mixed resonance.

Exercise 6: Rhythmic Scat Improvisation

Fuse rhythm + pitch + improvisation — the endgame

Groove Beat at 90 BPM. No guitar. Using syllables like "doo" "bah" "dee" "dah," improvise melodic lines that follow the chord tones of Am C G D.

Progression: (1) One syllable per beat — simple, locked in. (2) Syncopate — put syllables on the &'s. (3) 16th note bursts — use the "e and a" from Drill #3.

This directly connects the metronome subdivision work to vocal improvisation. You're not just keeping time — you're playing with where your voice sits INSIDE the beat.

Record a 2-minute improvisation. Listen back. Are your syncopations intentional or accidental? That's the diagnostic. The last word of each line in ILTWYW does exactly this kind of rhythmic play.

The Non-Negotiables
1

Always start with Metronome Drill #1. Sarah called this the most important exercise. It's the warm-up AND the foundation. Never skip it, even on rest day.

2

The nod is not optional. It's embodied rhythm. Your body needs to feel the downbeat physically. Down-nod on numbers, up-nod on &'s. Every. Single. Time.

3

Record yourself at least twice this week. Especially "I Like The Way You Walk" and the Sol Del Sur strum. Sarah wants a video of the strum. The recording is the mirror — you can't fix what you can't hear.

4

Don't simplify syncopation. If a word is supposed to land between beats, it needs to land between beats. The entire metronome apparatus serves this one outcome. Snapping to the grid is the easy path that leads nowhere.

5

COMMIT to notes. More air = better pitch. Tentative singing is the enemy of accurate singing. Sarah has said this across multiple lessons. Push air, even if it cracks. A committed crack is more useful than a safe whisper.

6

Pre-practice vocal warm-up is not optional. Lip trills → octave slides → straw phonation → humming. 8 minutes. Protects your voice and primes the passaggio for work. Avoid dairy before singing.

7

Listening homework counts as practice. 5 minutes of analytical listening (where do his words fall relative to the beat?) is worth more than 20 minutes of unfocused playing. Do it on the bus, in the shower, whenever.

8

Vowel modification through the break. "Ah" → "uh" approaching A3. "Ee" → "ih." This is the mechanical mechanism that enables mixed voice. Without it, you're just pushing chest voice until it breaks.
